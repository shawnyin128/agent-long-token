\begin{abstract}
Prior work on multi-agent debate (Du et al., 2023) reports accuracy
improvements over single-agent baselines on math reasoning. Our RQ0
work showed that on Qwen2.5-7B + GSM8K, the +6.7pp debate advantage is
not attributable to information transfer between agents — under a
drop-all control where every prior message is blanked, debate accuracy
is preserved. The remaining hypothesis is token-matched majority
voting (Wang et al., 2023). This paper maps where, across (model,
difficulty, thinking) cells, debate produces a measurable benefit
\emph{over} a token-matched voting baseline. Sixteen open-source
cells (two models $\times$ four datasets $\times$ thinking on/off)
plus a six-cell prompt-robustness sub-grid are run through a paired
bootstrap; the phase diagram of $\Delta = \text{acc(debate)} -
\text{acc(voting)}$ vs.\ single-agent accuracy is the main result.
\end{abstract}

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Models and Datasets}
Two open-source instruction-tuned models with native thinking
toggles:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Qwen3-30B-A3B} -- hybrid reasoning, exposes
        \texttt{enable\_thinking} via chat-template kwargs.
  \item \textbf{GPT-OSS-20B} -- supports
        \texttt{reasoning\_effort=low|high} per request.
\end{itemize}
Four datasets (cap 80 questions each):
GSM8K (easy math), AIME 2024+2025+2026 pool (hard math; per spec
§4.2.1, 20 from 2024 with seed 42 + 30 each from 2025/2026 to bound
contamination), HumanEval+ (easy code), and LiveCodeBench
(\texttt{contest\_date} $\geq$ 2024-08, hard code). All artifacts are
written under \texttt{artifacts/grid/\{cell\_dir\}/}.

\subsection{Conditions}
Per (model, dataset, thinking) cell we run three conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{SA} -- single-agent, $T = 0$, one inference per question.
  \item \textbf{Voting baseline} -- token-matched self-consistency
        (Wang et al., 2023): $N$ independent samples at $T = 0.7$,
        $\text{top-}p = 0.95$. $N$ is calibrated to total-token parity
        with debate from the first 10 questions, with a floor of
        $N \geq 3$ to preserve majority semantics.
  \item \textbf{$3 \times 3$ debate} -- three role-differentiated
        agents (solver / skeptic / synthesizer for math; proposer /
        reviewer / integrator for code) at $T = 0$. Each agent's
        thinking trace is internal; only the per-turn message is
        broadcast.
\end{enumerate}
Cells whose voting baseline received \emph{more} compute than debate
($\text{over\_budget\_factor} > 1.2$) are flagged in the phase diagram
with open markers; this strengthens any null result.

\subsection{Statistics}
Per cell, $\Delta = \text{acc(debate)} - \text{acc(voting)}$ is reported
with a paired-bootstrap 95\% CI ($n = 10000$, seed 42). Pairs are
matched by question index across conditions.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

(Per spec §7.)
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Math middle-difficulty band uncovered.} GSM8K (easy)
        $\to$ AIME (hard) skips MATH-500.
  \item \textbf{AIME contamination layered.} AIME 2024 (20/80) is
        likely in pretraining; 2025 (30) borderline; 2026 (30) clean.
        Mitigated by per-year stratification.
  \item \textbf{Two open models is a small basis.} Cross-model
        agreement table flags single-model artifacts where present.
  \item \textbf{Single seed per condition.} Bootstrap is over the
        question set, not over multiple generation seeds.
  \item \textbf{Diversity-source asymmetry.} Voting's diversity comes
        from temperature 0.7 sampling (Wang et al., 2023); debate's
        comes from differentiated role prompts at $T = 0$ (Du et al.,
        2023). Both follow their literature default; the small
        unidentified component is a known caveat.
  \item \textbf{Thinking-trace sharing fixed.} Agents' internal
        traces are not shared between debate rounds; this is a
        future ablation.
  \item \textbf{Cross-model token comparisons are nominal.} Different
        tokenizers carry different content per token; matched-compute
        comparisons across models are nominal alignments at the
        token-count level.
  \item \textbf{One debate protocol.} $3 \times 3$, role-differentiated.
        Other configurations are out of scope.
\end{itemize}
